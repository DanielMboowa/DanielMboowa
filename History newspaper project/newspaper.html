War Erupts in Europe: Germany Invades Poland
By Edward R. Murrow | Warsaw, Poland

In a shocking move, Nazi Germany, led by Adolf Hitler, launched a full-scale invasion of Poland on September 1, 1939. Using the Blitzkrieg (lightning war) strategy, German forces quickly advanced, bombing cities and overwhelming Polish defenses.

"The world stands at the brink of war. We will defend freedom against tyranny." — British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain ()

In response, Britain and France declared war on Germany today, marking the official start of World War II. The conflict has been brewing since Germany's annexation of Czechoslovakia earlier this year, but this unprovoked attack on Poland has forced the Allies to act.

German troops march into Poland, September 1939 (Bundesarchiv)

Eyewitnesses report devastating Luftwaffe bombing raids on Warsaw, with civilian casualties mounting. The Polish army, though valiant, is outmatched by Germany's modern tanks and aircraft. France has begun mobilizing its forces along the Maginot Line, while Britain prepares its Royal Air Force for possible attacks.

Allied vs. Axis Powers Take Shape

As nations take sides, the conflict is rapidly escalating:

Axis Powers: Germany, Italy, and Japan unite in aggression.

Allied Powers: Britain, France, and Poland stand together, with hopes for support from other nations.

Fear Spreads Across Europe

Citizens in multiple countries brace for uncertain times as military forces mobilize. There is growing concern that this war will surpass the devastation of World War I, with new technologies and deadlier weapons in play.

Pacific Theater
Victory at Midway: Turning the Tide
Midway Atoll, Pacific Ocean

In a major victory for the United States, the Battle of Midway, fought from June 3–7, 1942, has crippled the Japanese fleet. The battle, led by Admiral Chester W. Nimitz, saw U.S. forces destroy four Japanese aircraft carriers, shifting the balance of power in the Pacific War.

"Midway was the most crucial battle of the Pacific War." — Admiral Chester W. Nimitz (Tucker)

Japanese forces, under Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto, attempted to capture Midway Island as part of their plan to dominate the Pacific. However, U.S. intelligence cracked Japan's codes, allowing American forces to ambush the enemy fleet.

The U.S. aircraft carrier Yorktown shortly after being struck by Japanese bombs on June 4, 1942 (Naval Historical Center)

By June 7, Japan had lost four carriers, over 300 planes, and thousands of sailors, forcing them onto the defensive for the rest of the war. The victory at Midway proved that Japan's dominance in the Pacific could be challenged, paving the way for U.S. offensives toward final victory.

European Theater
Dunkirk: A Homefront Victory
📅 June 1940 | London, England

The Dunkirk evacuation, known as Operation Dynamo, saved over 300,000 British and Allied troops, bringing them home to fight another day. As German forces rapidly took France, British troops were trapped on the shores of Dunkirk.

"Wars are not won by evacuations." — Prime Minister Winston Churchill (Dunkirk, pp. 896-898)

Thanks to a heroic effort by the Royal Navy and civilian volunteers, soldiers were ferried across the English Channel, escaping disaster. Despite this warning from Churchill, the successful rescue boosted morale and gave Britain strength to continue the fight.

British troops awaiting evacuation at Dunkirk, June 1940 (Imperial War Museum)
The People's Role in Dunkirk

The Royal Navy, backed by civilian boats—fishing vessels, pleasure cruisers, and merchant ships—worked tirelessly to bring soldiers home. Many volunteers risked their lives, sailing through gunfire to reach stranded troops.

Meanwhile, on the homefront, British citizens welcomed the returning troops, providing food, shelter, and supplies. This event motivated Britain, strengthening its resolve to fight on without surrender.

War Crimes & Justice
The Nuremberg Trials: A Landmark in Justice
📅 October 1946 | 📍 Nuremberg, Germany

In a historic moment for international law, the Nuremberg Trials held Nazi leaders accountable for their crimes during World War II. These trials, which began on November 20, 1945, set a new precedent by making political leaders criminally responsible for their actions.

U.S. prosecutor Robert Jackson (left) and Soviet assistant prosecutor Colonel Yuri Pokrovsky at the Nuremberg Trials, October 1946 (Bettmann/Corbis)

At first, many doubted that the trials would happen. The British Foreign Office believed that Nazi crimes were too horrific for ordinary courts. However, the United States War Department pushed for a tribunal, arguing that justice must be carried out publicly.

Judges & Charges

The tribunal had eight judges, two from each Allied power:

United States: Francis Biddle, John Parker

United Kingdom: Sir Geoffrey Lawrence, Norman Birkett

Soviet Union: Iona Nikitchenko, Alexander Volchkov

France: Henri Donnedieu de Vabres, Robert Falco

Robert Jackson, a U.S. Supreme Court justice, led the prosecution. Twenty-two Nazi officials were prosecuted, including Hermann Goering, Rudolf Hess, and Joachim von Ribbentrop.

Historic Verdicts & Legacy

The trials ended on September 30, 1946. Twelve defendants, including Goering, were sentenced to death. Others received life or long prison sentences, while three were acquitted.

The Nuremberg Trials changed global law. They introduced the idea that leaders could be held accountable for crimes against humanity, no matter their official position.
